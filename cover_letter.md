# Cover Letter — Submission to the Journal of Sensor and Actuator Networks (JSAN)

**To:** Prof. Dr. Lei Shu, Editor-in-Chief, *Journal of Sensor and Actuator Networks*
**Manuscript:** *STGen: A Lightweight Sensor Testbed for Scenario-Based IoT Protocol Evaluation*

Dear Prof. Shu,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, *STGen: A Lightweight Sensor Testbed for Scenario-Based IoT Protocol Evaluation*, for consideration in the *Journal of Sensor and Actuator Networks*. STGen is a pure-software testbed for evaluating IoT application- and transport-layer protocols at the scale of thousands of sensor nodes on a single commodity workstation, and we believe its contributions align closely with the journal's focus on the science and technology of sensor and actuator networks.

**Significance and context.** Evaluating IoT protocols today forces a difficult choice. Physical testbeds such as FIT IoT-LAB and SmartSantander are faithful but costly and hard to scale; packet-level simulators such as ns-3, OMNeT++, and Cooja model the PHY/MAC layers well but demand deep expertise and do not target application-layer protocol logic at scale; and container/VM-based testbeds such as Gotham and GothX raise node counts only at a per-node cost of tens to hundreds of megabytes, capping a commodity host at a few hundred devices. STGen occupies the complementary point in this design space: it runs each emulated node as an independent operating-system process at a marginal **99 KB per node**, instantiating **6,000 concurrent nodes in 1.02 s using 0.62 GB of memory** — more than two orders of magnitude less memory per node than virtualization-based testbeds.

Crucially, STGen's sensor streams are not merely plausible: they are **calibrated against 1,826,223 real readings from the Intel Berkeley Research Laboratory** and validated statistically. For temperature, the synthetic and measured marginals agree to a Kolmogorov–Smirnov *D* of 0.071 and a Jensen–Shannon divergence of 0.018, the autocorrelation stays within 0.035 of the measured curve at every lag, and the temperature–humidity coupling is reproduced at *r* = −0.403 against the measured −0.426. A three-tier protocol/scenario/network decomposition reduces experiment specification from a multiplicative *N × M × L* to an additive *N + M + L*, and any conforming protocol integrates through a four-method abstract interface (demonstrated on MQTT, CoAP, and a custom UDP protocol).

Beyond scalability, STGen surfaces deployment behavior that controlled emulation hides: over a live wide-area overlay, the MQTT/CoAP loss ordering **inverts** relative to the emulated benchmark, and MQTT exhibits reconnect storms under bursty jitter. Finally, by inverting its calibrated models, STGen synthesizes **labelled false-data-injection anomalies across seven sensor modalities and six attack families**, separable from normal traffic at an ROC AUC of 1.0 for hard physical-range violations and a cross-modality mean of 0.90 for stealthy drift, while honestly reporting the temporal anomalies a value-domain detector cannot separate. Every table and figure is regenerated by a named script, and the central protocol result is confirmed by a large-sample Mann–Whitney test (*p* < 0.001).

**Fit with JSAN.** The manuscript concerns sensor-network emulation, physically grounded sensor-data modeling, protocol evaluation under realistic network conditions, and labelled dataset generation for intrusion detection — all central themes of JSAN. We expect it to be of direct interest to readers working on WSN testbeds, IoT protocol design (MQTT, CoAP, and emerging transports), and sensor-data security.

We confirm that neither the manuscript nor any parts of its content are currently under consideration for publication with or published in another journal.

All authors have approved the manuscript and agree with its submission to JSAN.
